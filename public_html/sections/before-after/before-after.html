A 1’ drop in elevation of Lake Mead reduces power generation capacity by 5.7 megawatts (Bureau of Reclamation), and a 10’ drop in elevation costs the National Park Service $2.1 million to extend boat ramps (NPS Lake Mead National Recreation Area fact sheet, 2014).

Hoover Dam Pre-Sedimentation Tank, located on Boulder Island in Lake Mead. Also called the Batch Plant or Water Clarifying Tank, the structure filtered water for washing gravel used in concrete during the construction of Hoover Dam. At one time about 80’ under water, the tank is approximately 110 feet in diameter, its walls rising 15 feet, with the elevation of the top of the tank at 1125’. It submerged during the filling of Lake Mead during the 1930s, and resurfaced in 2004 as lake levels fell.

Declining water elevations have affected recreation at Lake Mead, with boat ramps being extended and marinas being moved to deeper water. The National Park service, responsible for Lake Mead National Recreation Area, has been extending boat ramps since 2001, and planned to spend $5 million extending ramps in 2015. In 2008, Lake Mead Marina was moved from the location it had occupied since the 1960s. In 2007, another lake marina at Overton Beach split to join two other nearby marinas (Callville Bay Marina and the Temple Bar Marina).
