When the 1922 Colorado River Compact was completed and the allocations for Upper Basin and Lower Basin were developed, the average annual natural flow in the Upper Basin was about 18.0 MAF; when the 1944 U.S.-Mexico Water Treaty was completed, the average annual natural flow in the Upper Basin was about 16.3 MAF. Through water year 2012, the average annual natural inflow in the Upper Basin is about 14.8 MAF.

While the average annual inflow has decreased, the Colorado River system has been operated on an increasingly tight budget, and it now appears that the Colorado River system is over-allocated. The storage capacity available on the Colorado River system has allowed us to meet water demands during this sustained, 15-year drought. However, water use during the drought has exceeded supplies into the system, drawing down the stored reserves.

This trend in decreased water supply, coupled with increased water demand, emphasizes the need for all water users to proactively work together to reduce the strain of this tightening water budget.

When water use is greater than supply, water storage is decreased as can be seen in reduced levels in reservoirs in the Upper (link to UC Basin Teacup Diagram) and Lower Colorado River basin (link to LC Basin Teacup Diagram).
